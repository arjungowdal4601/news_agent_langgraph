# 2025 BMW M2 Yearlong Review Verdict: The Ultimate One Car Solution

After crowning the BMW M2 our Performance Vehicle of the Year, we wanted to know how well it navigated daily life. A year and 17,000 miles later, we have our answer.

We began our year behind the wheel of the 2025 [ BMW M2](https://www.motortrend.com/cars/bmw/m2) with a fair dose of skepticism. Despite its humble beginnings as a Frankenstein mishmash of components from the BMW parts catalog, the compact sports car had proved itself far and beyond what we thought was possible. It was so visceral and thrilling to drive and punched so far above its weight, it ultimately scored a win over heavy hitters from Lamborghini, Porsche, and Ferrari at what would be our final Performance Vehicle of the Year award.

## So why the skepticism?

While wide-ranging, our previous testing of the M2 was largely focused on how well it played the role of a sports car, where downsides such as small (or nonexistent) back seats, tiny trunks, or poor fuel economy aren’t given the same weight as, say, powertrain performance, steering sharpness, chassis balance, or sheer fun-to-drive character.

A yearlong test, on the other hand, is an entirely different animal. There are the pointless commutes to the office for bad coffee and communal germs, weekend errands, doctor’s appointments and vet visits, a crosstown excursion to pick up a statue that looked far smaller on Facebook Marketplace, or a multiweek road trip. These aren’t the types of use cases in which sports cars typically thrive. And yet over 14 months and 17,042 miles, our 2025 BMW M2 did just that.

## What We Got and What We Did With Our M2

One of the aspects that helped the BMW M2 cement its Performance Vehicle of the Year win back in 2025 was how well equipped it comes from the factory. Unlike, say, Porsche or Ford, BMW ships every M2 with all the performance goodies you could conceivably need. Under the hood is a potent 3.0-liter twin-turbo I-6 with 473 hp and 406 lb-ft of torque driving the rear-wheels via a six-speed manual gearbox (or 443 lb-ft if you choose the eight-speed automatic) and an electronically controlled limited-slip differential. Six-piston 15.0-inch front and 14.6-inch floating Brembo brakes slow things down, while an adaptive suspension setup and some seriously impressive (and most importantly transparent) software help tie it all together.

Even so, we couldn’t resist the allure of the option sheet, adding some style in the form of our M2’s alluring Twilight Metallic Purple paint and carbon-fiber roof, and some function in the form of a head-up display, adaptive LED lights, heated steering wheel, parking sensors, and a wireless phone charger. We also added the M Driver’s pack for its top-speed boost and ticket to the M Performance driving school. The base price for our M2 was $66,075, with our options bumping the out-the-door number up to $77,325. For 2026, an identically optioned M2 will cost you $81,600.

That proved to be all the car we needed.

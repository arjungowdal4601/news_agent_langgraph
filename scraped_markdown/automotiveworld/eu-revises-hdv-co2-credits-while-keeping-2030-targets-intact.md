The European Council has formally adopted an amendment to EU CO2 emission standards for heavy-duty vehicles, introducing temporary flexibility in how manufacturers can accumulate emission credits ahead of the 2030 reduction targets. The change, part of the European Commission’s automotive package announced in December 2025, does not alter the underlying targets of a 43% CO2 reduction from 2030 and 90% from 2040.

Under the revised rules, manufacturers of heavy lorries over 16 tonnes and certain bus categories over 7.5 tonnes can accumulate credits between 2025 and 2029 when their fleet emissions fall below their own annual CO2 targets, rather than against the stricter linear reduction trajectory connecting five-year milestones. The mechanism is intended to incentivise earlier deployment of zero-emission vehicles and ease compliance from 2030 onwards. Urban buses are excluded from the flexibility, as zero-emission deployment in that segment is already advanced.

The Council cited slow rollout of public motorway charging infrastructure as a key factor behind the amendment. In a statement, Maria Panayiotou, Minister for Agriculture, Rural Development and Environment of the Republic of Cyprus, said: “With this timely and pragmatic amendment, we give heavy-duty vehicle manufacturers and investors the flexibility and predictability they need to navigate this transition, while electric charging stations are being deployed across Europe.”

The regulation will be published in the Official Journal of the EU and enter into force 20 days thereafter. The broader CO2 standards framework for heavy-duty vehicles is due for revision in 2027.

BYD’s Japan chief, Atsuki Tofukuji, has warned that the automaker cannot compete in the Japanese market under its existing subsidy framework, which leaves all four of its local models receiving between JP¥350,000–JP¥450,000 (US$2,200–US$2,800) in purchase assistance. This equates to as much as JP¥950,000 less than comparable models from domestic marques like Toyota vehicles, following a revision that took effect in January 2026.

“We’re at an overwhelming disadvantage,” Tofukuji said in a 17 March interview with *Nikkei*. “The gap has grown to up to nearly one million yen. We can’t compete with ¥350,000.” The Japanese government’s January revisions saw the clean energy vehicle subsidy ceiling be raised from JP¥900,000 to a significantly higher JP¥1.3m. The change, clearly designed to curry favour with the US amid trade negotiations, was also intended to close the gap between electrified and fuel cell vehicle incentives.

The revision increased the ceiling but left actual payouts determined by a 200-point scoring system introduced back in 2024, which weights vehicle performance alongside a company evaluation covering a range of criteria including charging infrastructure, maintenance networks, disaster response capability, and cyber security.

BYD’s scores on the company evaluation have remained low despite extensive efforts to build out its local dealership and fast-charging networks. Tofukuji said BYD received zero points for charging infrastructure development even as it installs fast chargers at dealerships nationwide, and that the relevant ministry declined to explain the scoring when asked.

“If the reason is just because we’re a Chinese manufacturer, then I want them to say so,” he remarked. BYD has made significant efforts to ingratiate itself to historically-reluctant Japanese consumers—perhaps most visibly with the [reveal](https://www.automotiveworld.com/topics/e-mobility/byd-teases-kei-car-prototype-ahead-of-japan-debut/) of its first-ever market-exclusive model, the Racco kei electric vehicle.

Keen to assuage Japanese consumers’ doubts about purchasing non-Japanese models—especially those from Chinese marques—BYD has taken a range of measures to [present itself](https://www.automotiveworld.com/news/byd-articulates-its-japan-kei-segment-strategy/) as a brand that ‘feels local’, even if it actually is not. This includes its choice of dealership location: instead of large lots in urban outskirts retail parks, it is instead opting for neighbourhood malls and smaller, more rural locations. It planned to launch 100 local dealerships by end-2026, but fell short of this target at around 69 instead.

Given the automaker’s efforts, particularly on the charging front, it is noteworthy that Western automakers fared altogether better under the revised subsidy scheme. Tesla’s subsidy rose by JP¥400,000 to ¥1.27m under the same framework, a result attributed in part to its Supercharger network earning points under the infrastructure criteria. Hyundai also saw increases for some models. The Japanese government maintains that subsidies were increased for vehicles receiving high overall green transformation scores, and that it responds seriously to all enquiries from automakers.

The appearance of preferential treatment for non-Chinese OEMs has not gone unnoticed by industry analysts. The January update was partly shaped by US-Japan trade discussions, and the preferential outcome for Tesla has not gone unnoticed. “As expected, the American players got favourable treatment,” one industry figure affiliated with imported vehicles told *Nikkei*.

Yoshiaki Kawano at S&P Global Mobility noted to the outlet that while the core evaluation criteria themselves had not changed, the revision had produced inconsistencies across companies. “The change came after the U.S.-Japan tariff negotiations, so it can’t be ruled out that some aspects could be perceived as favorable treatment from the government,” Kawano said.

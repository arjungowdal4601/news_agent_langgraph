The 2027 Mercedes-Benz EQS Fixes Its Biggest Problems—But Is It Enough?

Open Source
  • Mercedes’ 2027 EQS gets a major hardware reset after a lukewarm launch: 800-volt electrical architecture, new battery chemistry, new motors, and other “running-gear lift” changes aimed at fixing slow charging and the car’s prior 400-volt bottleneck.
  • Though the V297 EQS still rides on its original MEA EV architecture, it borrows MB.EA tech from the new electric 2027 GLC SUV and upcoming electric C-Class, including more efficient drive units and a rear two-speed transaxle.
  • The rear motor’s two-speed setup provides 11:1 torque multiplication at launch, then shifts to 5:1 gearing at nearly any speed up to 70-ish mph, helping sustain peak power to the 130-mph top speed.
  • Battery packaging improves via new silicon-oxide/graphite anodes and less-cobalt chemistry, lifting capacity to 122 kWh from 118 kWh; Mercedes says peak DC charging rises to 350 kW on the best towers and 175 kW on 400-volt equipment.
  • Regenerative braking is also quicker, with recuperation up to 385 kW and about one-third faster energy recovery.

Bosch’s Wild New Way to Clean Up Gas Engines Starts With a Flame

Open Source
  • Bosch’s Rapid Catalyst Heater targets the dirtiest part of a drive—the cold start—while criteria emissions remain fully regulated even as some CO2 rules are rolled back; the company says the system could make gas cars cleaner and PHEVs much cleaner.
  • Modern three-way catalysts can remove up to 98% of criteria emissions once fully warm (750–1,100 degrees F), so EPA test success hinges on the first 20–60 seconds of the 31-minute dyno cycle; engineers typically use catalyst placement, rich startup, timing retard, secondary air injection, or direct electric heating to speed warmup.
  • Bosch’s new knob is a gas burner that can deliver almost instantly 25 kW of heating energy directly into the exhaust stream ahead of the catalyst—far more than typical 1–10 kW electric catalyst heaters.
  • The system uses a secondary air-injection pump, Bosch mass airflow sensor, low-pressure fuel through a Bosch port injector with a unique nozzle-hole pattern, a Bosch diesel glow plug for ignition, and a Bosch oxygen sensor targeting stoichiometric 14.7:1 air-fuel ratio before the exhaust enters the catalyst.

Aramco Built a Hybrid to Beat Toyota—And It’s Wildly Different

Open Source
  • Aramco, the Saudi Arabian Oil Company and world’s largest oil producer, has developed a Dedicated Hybrid Engine (DHE) to keep combustion relevant as hybrids outsell BEVs in some regions and to push a new hybrid “mousetrap” for future gas-hybrid demand.
  • The DHE is Aramco’s answer to turnkey hybridization systems like Horse Powertrain’s range-extender and engine offerings; Horse is the Renault/Geely-Volvo JV with 45% held by each parent and 10% by Aramco.
  • Aramco’s concept asks what an ideal hybrid powertrain would look like if designed from scratch, and its layout is Toyota-like in using a motor-generator/planetary-gear arrangement—except it uses one at each end of the crankshaft.
  • The proof-of-concept engine is a 1,599cc inline-three with a cam-in-block pushrod design, monoblock casting with no separate cylinder head, two valves per cylinder, no variable timing or lift, roller bearings for the crank/camshaft/conrods, a 12-mm crank offset to reduce piston side loading, and no accessory drive; water pump and auxiliaries run electrically on demand.

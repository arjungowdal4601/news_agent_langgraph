Best Tech 2026: The Lucid Gravity Proves That Great Fast-Charging Is About More Than Power

Open Source
  • Lucid’s engineers had to rethink the Gravity’s charging plan after Tesla opened Supercharger access to competitors in February 2023, by which point Grand Touring development was already about 70% complete; the SUV had been headed toward a 936-volt pack and 400-kW charging on 1,000-volt hardware, but at Tesla’s nearly 18,000 North American Superchargers—mostly 500-volt—the car would have been capped at 50 kW.
  • Emad Dlala, Lucid’s senior vice president of engineering and digital, said charging convenience is “almost the number one factor for EV adoption” and that Tesla’s Supercharger network “has made Tesla’s success,” prompting a rapid redesign in weeks rather than months.
  • The fix: swap the CCS port for a Tesla-style NACS port, move it from the driver’s-side front to the driver’s rear fender, and ditch the DC-to-DC converter by routing power through the rear motor’s windings to step 500-volt input up to the needed charging voltage.
  • That raised peak charging power to 225 kW on Tesla V3 stations, just under their 250-kW max, and helped the Gravity win a 2026 MotorTrend Best Tech award in Public Charging Experience; on the right hardware, the Gravity Grand Touring is MotorTrend’s fastest-charging SUV, adding 194 miles in 15 minutes, while its 400-kW peak comes from Panasonic-developed nickel-manganese-cobalt lithium-ion cells.
  • Lucid also tuned charging software using real-world Lucid Air usage data, not just lab testing, to push performance harder without battery damage.

Volkswagen’s 2027 Atlas Looks New—But Is It Actually Better?

Open Source
  • The 2027 Volkswagen Atlas is riding on an evolution of the MQB (modular transverse) platform with the same wheelbase and a lightly massaged EA888 engine, so it’s not a clean-sheet redesign even though the sheetmetal, interior and electrical architecture are new.
  • VW says the updated electrical architecture isn’t the centralized software-defined system Rivian designed for Volkswagen, but it does enable a new set of features and functionality.
  • Exterior changes include a longer nose for pedestrian protection, a glossy black grille that can look EV-like, twin light bars with six daytime running lamps per side, illuminated VW logos on all but the base trim, animated approach/depart lighting, and wheel sizes from 18 to 21 inches.
  • The Gen 5 turbo-four, first seen in the 2026 Volkswagen Tiguan, is now the Atlas’s most powerful yet at 282 hp and 258 lb-ft, up 13 hp and down 15 lb-ft versus last year; VW says a variable-geometry turbo inlet nozzle, Miller-cycle cam timing, and a more interconnected cooling system trade a slightly lower torque peak for quicker spool, similar performance, and better fuel economy on regular gas.

Here’s What Hyundai’s Boulder Needs to Fight the Jeep Wrangler and Ford Bronco

Open Source
  • Hyundai’s Boulder is still only a concept, but its reveal at the 2026 New York International Auto Show signals the brand is serious about the U.S. off-road market—and about targeting the Jeep Wrangler and Ford Bronco rather than a pickup like the Ford Ranger.
  • The body-on-frame SUV looks on the right track, but MotorTrend says production needs to respect off-road expectations while giving Hyundai room to modernize the formula, since Wrangler/Bronco heritage can be both an asset and a constraint.
  • For the base model, MotorTrend wants mechanical simplicity: a naturally aspirated V-6 or turbocharged I-4, a manual transmission, 35-inch tires as a starting point, and true driver-engageable mechanical differential lockers rather than ABS-based substitutes.
  • It also wants the Boulder to be a true 4WD SUV with a real transfer case, plus a shifter beside the transmission lever instead of buttons or knobs, to give drivers the mechanical feel and control off-road buyers expect.

Rivian sees “a ton of opportunity” in digital key tech

Open Source

Open Source

Audi introduces torque vectoring world first in RS 5

Open Source
  • Audi says the new RS 5 is the first production model with electromechanical rear-axle torque vectoring; the system, quattro with Dynamic Torque Control, is part of a modular plug-in hybrid drivetrain and can shift up to 2,000 Nm between the rear wheels in 15 milliseconds, independent of drivetrain load.
  • The rear transaxle is purpose-built for the RS 5 hybrid drive and includes a water-cooled 400-volt permanent-magnet motor producing 8 kW and 40 Nm as a high-voltage actuator; overdrive gears and a conventional differential with a low lock percentage work with the actuator to route torque individually, including off-throttle and under braking.
  • Audi says the HCP1 High-Performance Computing Platform coordinates drivetrain/suspension functions in real time using steering input, vehicle dynamics data and environmental conditions; the system also works with front-axle electronic differential lock, brake torque vectoring and twin-valve adaptive shock absorbers.
  • The RS 5 is offered in multiple Audi drive select modes, from neutral and balanced to rear-biased.

NTSB faults Ford’s ADAS tech in two fatal highway crashes

Open Source
  • The US NTSB says Ford’s BlueCruise failed to mitigate driver distraction in two fatal 2024 crashes and that neither would have been avoided even with the system’s updated version; the 31 March 2026 hearing also pressed the lack of federal performance standards for partial automation.
  • The crashes involved 2022 Ford Mustang Mach-E SUVs on BlueCruise on interstate highways in San Antonio (February 2024) and Philadelphia (one month later); both hit stationary cars at highway speed with no braking from driver or system, causing three fatalities.
  • NTSB said BlueCruise’s driver monitoring failed to detect inattention, and the system allowed drivers to disable automatic emergency braking and to drive up to 20 mph over the posted limit; Ford said it will take the recommendations under serious consideration, while noting one driver had been impaired by alcohol.
  • NHTSA, which has three open BlueCruise investigations, will review the findings; NTSB chair Jennifer Homendy said, “We cannot take a hands-off approach to hands-free driving technology. Lives depend on it.”
  • The case adds to NTSB scrutiny of SAE Level 2 systems, where automakers largely set their own monitoring/speed/data-recording rules, and the agency also pointed to its 2023 Tesla Autopilot probe and ongoing Waymo school-bus incidents.

Faster, simpler, smarter: how ETAS busts the AUTOSAR myth | Automotive World

Open Source
  • ETAS’s Darren Buttle argues AUTOSAR Classic’s complexity reputation is overstated; while the standard is often seen as slow and cumbersome, he says it remains valuable for deeply embedded ECU work, especially via ETAS’s RTA-CAR platform.
  • Buttle says AUTOSAR’s complexity stems from 25 years of contributions, coverage-first growth, and legacy-migration needs; at its core it solves runtime scheduling, reliable communication, reliable data storage and field diagnostics on bare-metal systems, acting as glue between varied hardware and OEM requirements.
  • He says implementation pain comes mainly from configuration scale—hundreds of thousands of elements and little guidance on the right choices—creating a semantic gap that adds time and risk; RTA-CAR aims to close that with faster configuration, code generation and workflow integration.
  • ETAS says RTA-CAR can cut build turnaround from about eight hours to a handful of minutes, enabling 20, 30 or even 50 changes per day, while also bringing decades of embedded expertise to guide customers in the right direction.
  • The platform’s optimisations target space/time trade-offs, slack-time/RAM balancing, code-generation efficiency and virtual ECU workflows; ETAS says its solutions can be three to four times smaller than competing offerings, and its tooling helps with multicore data exchange, serialisation, mutual exclusion and data-placement timing.

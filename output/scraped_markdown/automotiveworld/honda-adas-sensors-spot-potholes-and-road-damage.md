Honda and the Ohio Department of Transportation (ODOT) have completed a pilot project that used advanced driver assistance system (ADAS) sensors fitted to Honda vehicles to detect road defects in real time. The automaker said the system monitored approximately 3,000 miles of roads in central and southeastern Ohio using existing vision cameras and LiDAR.

![](https://media.automotiveworld.com/app/uploads/2026/04/16075300/honda-safety-graphic-1024x237.jpg)


The prototype Honda Proactive Roadway Maintenance System identified potholes, missing signs, damaged guardrails and other hazards, achieving 93% accuracy for guardrail damage and 89% for potholes. Detected defects were processed by artificial intelligence (AI) and sent automatically to ODOT dashboards, where work orders were generated and prioritised by severity. Honda estimates that scaled deployment could save ODOT more than US$4.5m a year.

![](https://media.automotiveworld.com/app/uploads/2026/04/16075252/honda-pilot-vehicle-system-odot-proactive-roadway-maintenance-project-1024x576.jpg)


The project was developed alongside ODOT’s smart mobility hub DriveOhio, technology partners i-Probe Inc. and Parsons, and the University of Cincinnati. Honda said it is now in active discussions with several US states about expanding the system to other transport departments.

![](https://media.automotiveworld.com/app/uploads/2026/04/16075255/honda-proactive-roadway-maintenance.jpg)


Brian Bautsch, Director of Safety Strategy at American Honda Motor Co., said: “We can’t achieve zero fatalities through technology alone.

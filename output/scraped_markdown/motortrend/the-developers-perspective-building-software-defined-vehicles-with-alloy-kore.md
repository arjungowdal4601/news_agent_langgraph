__Alloy Kore__## The Scaling Crisis

In the early days of automotive software development, everything was foundational. Individual hardware components handled discrete functions in cars, and none were even aware of each other. Your windshield wiper controller never talked to your headlight controllers, for example. Discrete components handling their own tasks meant that writing software for each was relatively straightforward.

Over the years, that’s changed, with more and more systems becoming interrelated and controlled by shared systems and controllers. Zonal architectures or central architecture with HPCs as the focus are becoming the norm, with systems like BMW's Heart of Joy, Mercedes MB.OS*,* or Rivian's second-gen platform consolidating onto fewer, more powerful systems sharing information and collaborating.

The challenge, though, comes in scaling a system fused together using different software pieces from different vendors. "Progress towards the SDV has slowed down due to fragmented software integration. Integrating disparate components leads to unpredictable behavior, costly rework and an increased risk of missed production deadlines and recalls," explained Max-Ferdinand Suffel, a workflow architect at Vector who works on the company’s software platform offering, and part of the development team shaping the [ Alloy Kore](https://src.vector.com/l/38be7eb1) platform.

And it's only going to get worse. Once upon a time, cars never changed after pulling off the lot. Now, they're expected to stay updateable for much longer. "Today, you want to continuously be adding features later on, and this may be for 10, 15 years," Suffel said.

It goes beyond features, too. Car companies must also provide security and safety updates for that decade or more, a shift that extends well beyond technical challenges. "The entire software development and engineering organization has to evolve," Suffel noted.

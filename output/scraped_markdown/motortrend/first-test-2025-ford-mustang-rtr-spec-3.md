# 2025 Ford Mustang RTR Spec 3 First Test: What We Expected From a Drift Champion’s Shop

Formula Drift tuning, rear-wheel drive, and 810 horsepower equal one slidey Mustang.

[Scott Evans](https://www.motortrend.com/staff/scott-evans)Writer

[William Walker](https://www.motortrend.com/staff/william-walker)Photographer

### Pros

- Rips from a roll
- Drifts on command
- Tasteful body mods

### Cons

- Can’t put the power down
- Handful at the limit
- A $45,000 interior in a $107,000 car

When offered the opportunity to test [ Ford Mustangs](https://www.motortrend.com/cars/ford/mustang) modified by two-time Formula Drift champion Vaughn Gitten Jr.’s shop, RTR, we made some assumptions. The top-of-the-line Spec 5 we tested first destroyed them. The more affordable, more readily available RTR Spec 3 we tested next, though, confirmed every single one of them.

0:00 / 0:00

Our primary assumption, quite simply, was that a tuner car built by a pro drifter’s shop would be able to get sideways at the drop of a hat. But the Spec 5, with its extra-wide wheels, stickier tires, and grip-oriented suspension setup, didn’t meet said assumption. Despite its tremendous power and rear-drive configuration, that car wanted to grip and go. Getting it to drift took real effort, and even then, they weren’t huge, smokey drifts. The Mustang RTR Spec 3, despite using most of the same parts, proved the opposite.

## What’s a Spec 3?

RTR builds three different versions of the Ford Mustang, Spec 2, Spec 3, and Spec 5, in increasing order of performance, price, and exclusivity. [ Spec 2 is all handling and cosmetic upgrades](https://www.motortrend.com/reviews/1705-reviewing-rtrs-2016-mustang-spec-2), Spec 3 adds a Ford Racing supercharger, and Spec 5 adds a custom widebody kit and Borla exhaust. (No, there’s no Spec 1 or 4). Each starts life as a Mustang GT coupe with the 5.0-liter V-8 and the Performance package.

The middle-child Spec 3 gets the same RTR Tactical Performance 30-way adjustable shocks, coilover-lowering springs, and three-way-adjustable anti-roll bars as the other cars. A Whipple 3.0-liter supercharger certified by Ford Racing is bolted onto the stock 5.0-liter V-8 and jacks its output to 810 hp and 615 lb-ft of torque without requiring an engine or transmission rebuild, nor a new clutch or rear end. For emissions legality, California customers will have to have the supercharger kit installed by a local RTR-authorized dealer. Everyone else can get it installed by RTR itself in its North Carolina shop. The setup is available for both the six-speed manual and 10-speed automatic.

All that power goes to a set of staggered-width, 20-inch RTR wheels wrapped in Nitto NT555 G2 ultra-high-performance summer tires. Despite all the extra power, the brakes are not upgraded.

Most other modifications are cosmetic, and there are a lot of them. The chin splitter, rocker extensions, and decklid spoiler may do a bit to improve aerodynamics, but the rest is for show. We like the signature triangular nostril running lights in the grille, but we could do without the sticker package, which peels off way too easily.

# Toyota Land Cruiser Yearlong Test Update: It Needs a Windshield Budget

Two windshield replacements in less than a year cost us more than $4,000, and that doesn’t count the rock chip we got filled.

[Scott Evans](https://www.motortrend.com/staff/scott-evans)Writer

[William Walker](https://www.motortrend.com/staff/william-walker)Photographer

![](https://hips.hearstapps.com/hmg-prod/images/e0fae3ba-9aca-4f44-9de1-3ec521ca4411.jpg?w=768&width=768&q=75&format=webp)

Of all the bad sounds out there, glass breaking ranks near the top. Doubly so when you’re on the freeway and you catch a rock in your windshield. First, it’s the sudden *crack!*, quickly followed by the awful realization you’ve just been signed up for a potentially expensive, completely unexpected, and definitely unwanted repair. It’s happened to us three times in less than a year driving our long-term [ 2025 Toyota Land Cruiser](http://motortrend.com/cars/toyota/land-cruiser), and it’s getting really old.

## Rock Magnet

The first time wasn’t entirely unanticipated. It happened [ on the Dalton Highway in Alaska](https://www.motortrend.com/reviews/2025-toyota-land-crusier-yearlong-review-update-4/photos), a 414-mile dirt and gravel road mostly frequented by big rigs. Catching a rock or three there is almost inevitable, so we weren’t surprised when the Land Cruiser came back to California with three separate star patterns in the windshield, plus a few cracks.

Off to our local dealership it went, where the work was farmed out to a local glass and tint shop that charged us $1,691.58 for a new windshield and recalibration of the forward-looking camera behind it. That was late August.

By the middle of September, the Land Cruiser had already picked up a big chip in its brand-new windshield. This one came on Los Angeles’ Interstate 405 in light traffic, not even doing something cool. Dismayed and irritated, we put off getting it fixed until the next service came up in late October. The truck went to the same dealer and was sent off to the same glass shop, which filled the chip in the lower passenger corner for $88 and rendered the chip mostly invisible.

![](https://hips.hearstapps.com/hmg-prod/images/c4a3e33e-880a-4167-8433-d6e507cc32fe.jpg?w=768&width=768&q=75&format=webp)

The fix lasted a few months, until we were driving down Interstate 5 on the way home from visiting friends and caught another chip of equal size not 2 inches from the previous blemish. Further irritated, we decided to put off a fix until the next service, which turned out to be a smarter choice than we anticipated.

That’s because a few weeks later, we caught another rock in the top edge of the windshield directly in front of the driver’s seat, which quickly turned into a footlong crack. This one also occurred on a local freeway and not while off-roading or something else known to be extra dangerous to bodywork and glass.

Out of convenience, the Toyota went to a different local dealer this time for its third service and second windshield. This is where we learned between August of 2025 and March of 2026, the price of a Land Cruiser windshield went up by 40 percent. Our second windshield replacement and camera recalibration set us back $2,360.72.

For those keeping score, we’ve now spent $4,052.30 on new windshields, $4,140.30 if you include the rock chip we got filled (which we do, for bookkeeping purposes). All that in just 10 months and about 25,000 miles of ownership. If that sounds excessive to you, it is. We see plenty of chipped windshields around here, but we’re having a hard time remembering the last time we replaced a windshield, much less twice on the same vehicle.

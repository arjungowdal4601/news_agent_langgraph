Although the mere thought of transforming a gas-powered classic into an EV is downright blasphemous to most automotive purists, there are more than a few benefits to going electric, Kevin Emr, founder and president of E-Muscle Cars near Dallas, said. “Dropping in a modern, low-maintenance electric motor almost makes too much sense,” Emr said. “You get modern reliable power with no noise, no leaks, no odors, and little maintenance.”

EV conversions also have the potential to expand classic car ownership beyond just gearheads. “If you own a classic car, you know it comes with some character but also some labor,” James Fleischman, owner of Flash Drive Motors near Austin, Texas, said. He said there are more people who’d like to own a classic car but hesitate because they either don’t have the skills themselves or know of a shop to help them properly maintain one. “Converting to electric makes it accessible to those who just want to enjoy their classic car without worrying about finding someone to keep it running. You get used to a car that just works every time you want to drive it.”

While an EV conversion from a shop like E-Muscle Cars can easily reach into the six-figure range, the cost is largely on par with that of most high-end restomod builders. “A really nice restomod has the same amount poured into the wheels, tires, frame, motor, gearbox, paint, you name it,” Emr said. “Most of our conversion includes all that but will always cost more because of the batteries.”

That said, if you’re the DIY type, Flash Drive Motors and other EV conversion outfits also sell complete kits. “We make an entire line of products specifically designed to make it easy for weekend warriors to convert their own cars,” Fleischman said.

The major disadvantages of a classic car conversion to electricity are largely the same as with any EV: range, charging, and weight. Most EV conversion shops offer different battery packs and related componentry that will allow for varying levels of charging speeds and overall range. And an EV conversion will almost always weigh more because of the batteries, though not appreciably given that the engine, transmission, gas tank, and exhaust system are all removed. “All our conversions will always weigh 200 to 300 pounds more than an ICE car, but that’s just an extra passenger or two,” Emr said.

But depending on how much you’re willing to spend, one of the payoffs of converting a classic gas-powered car to an EV can be improved performance. “You have a smaller powerplant but get the same if not greater power than the original gas motor with instant torque,” Emr said. “It’s still muscle. In fact, it’s more muscle. Most of our cars make 400 to 600 lb-ft of torque, and there’s no shiftable transmission, so our cars make high torque at all times. It’s like a big-block in first gear, but you’re in first gear all the time.”

## Audi introduces torque vectoring world first in RS 5

Source URL: <https://www.automotiveworld.com/news/audi-introduces-torque-vectoring-world-first-in-rs-5/>

- Audi has introduced electromechanical torque vectoring at the rear axle of the new RS 5, which it describes as a world first in a production model.
- The system, called quattro with Dynamic Torque Control, is part of a modular plug-in hybrid drivetrain and can distribute torque differences of up to 2,000 Nm between the rear wheels in 15 milliseconds, operating independently of drivetrain load.
- The rear transaxle is purpose-designed for the RS 5’s hybrid drive and houses a water-cooled 400-volt permanent-magnet electric motor producing 8 kW and 40 Nm, acting as a high-voltage actuator. Overdrive gears and a conventional differential with a low lock percentage work with the actuator to direct torque to individual rear wheels.
- Unlike clutch-based torque splitters, the system functions when off-throttle and under braking, giving it a broader operational envelope than previous approaches.
- Audi says the system is managed by the HCP1 High-Performance Computing Platform, the central control unit for drivetrain and suspension functions, integrating steering input, vehicle dynamics data and environmental conditions to coordinate torque distribution in real time.
- The RS 5’s electromechanical torque vectoring works alongside a front-axle electronic differential lock, brake torque vectoring, and twin-valve adaptive shock absorbers calibrated to the rear system; it is offered across multiple Audi drive select modes, from neutral and balanced to rear-biased configurations.

## NTSB faults Ford’s ADAS tech in two fatal highway crashes

Source URL: <https://www.automotiveworld.com/news/ntsb-faults-fords-adas-tech-in-two-fatal-highway-crashes/>

- The US National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB) concluded that Ford’s [BlueCruise](https://www.automotiveworld.com/topics/software-defined-vehicle/ford-to-expand-bluecruise-to-four-additional-models/) ADAS failed to mitigate driver distraction in two fatal 2024 crashes, and that neither incident would have been avoided even under the system’s updated version.
- The findings were delivered at a three-hour hearing on 31 March 2026 and also criticised the absence of federal performance standards governing partial automation systems, urging regulators to close the gap.
- Both crashes involved 2022 Ford Mustang Mach-E SUVs on BlueCruise on interstate highways — one in San Antonio in February 2024, the other in Philadelphia one month later. In each case, the vehicle struck stationary cars at highway speed with no braking recorded from either the driver or the system, causing three fatalities in total.
- NTSB found BlueCruise’s driver monitoring failed to detect inattention in both instances, and the system allowed drivers to disable automatic emergency braking and exceed the posted limit by up to 20 mph.
- Ford said it would take the recommendations under serious consideration, while noting that one of the two drivers had been impaired by alcohol. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, which has three open investigations into BlueCruise, said it would review the findings.
- NTSB chair Jennifer Homendy said: “We cannot take a hands-off approach to hands-free driving technology. Lives depend on it.” The agency said the lack of a federal framework for SAE Level 2 systems has left automakers to set their own monitoring, speed limit and data-recording parameters, and it called on the Department of Transportation to establish standardised performance requirements.
- The findings add to prior scrutiny of partial automation, including a 2023 Tesla Autopilot investigation that prompted a recall of two million vehicles. NTSB is also investigating fully autonomous operations, including Waymo robotaxi incidents involving illegally passing stopped school buses.
